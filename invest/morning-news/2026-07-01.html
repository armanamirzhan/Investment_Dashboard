WARSH AT SINTRA — Fed Chair says 'AI shock is leading to a boom in capital expenditures'; supply-side expansion has 'huge implications for monetary policy' but reiterates prices 'too high'; declines to signal July FOMC action; markets fade Nasdaq futures on lack of dovish tilt — Fed Chair Kevin Warsh made his international debut Wednesday July 1 at the ECB Forum in Sintra, sharing a panel with ECB's Lagarde, BoE's Bailey, and BoC's Macklem. Key takeaways: (a) AI framed as the dominant supply-side story — 'the AI shock is leading to a boom in capital expenditures. We see that first and foremost in demand, but I'm confident we're going to see it in supply at some point'; (b) 'Right now they're investing in the future because their expectation is the supply side of the economy will expand, and if it does, that has huge implications for monetary policy' — hint that Fed sees AI capex as disinflationary through capacity-build; (c) declined to give any July-FOMC signal but reiterated 'prices are too high'; (d) reinforced his critique of Fed 'mission creep' and exit from forward guidance. US500 opened -0.38% at 7,471 as traders had priced a more dovish tone. Read-through to House coverage: (1) POSITIVE STRUCTURAL for the entire AI-infrastructure cohort — Warsh explicitly endorsed the AI-capex thesis as a monetary-policy-relevant supply-side variable, elevating hyperscaler/power/semi capex as a Fed reaction-function input; (2) NEGATIVE NEAR-TERM for high-multiple software (PLTR, NOW, CRM, SNOW, CRWD) — no rate-cut confirmation leaves the discount-rate overhang in place; (3) POSITIVE for durable AI-infra picks-and-shovels (NVDA, AVGO, AMAT, LRCX, KLAC, ETN, GEV, VRT, CEG, VST) — the capex cycle is now Fed-endorsed rather than viewed as bubble excess; (4) sets up a bifurcated H2 setup: rate-sensitive software waits on cuts, AI-infra hardware/power gets structural bid.
