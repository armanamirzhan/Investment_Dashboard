Investment Thesis

Constellation Energy is the world's largest private-sector power producer with 55 GW of generation capacity following the transformational $16.4B acquisition of Calpine Corporation (closed January 7, 2026). The combined company pairs Constellation's industry-leading 22 GW nuclear fleet (183 TWh of zero-emissions electricity in 2025) with Calpine's 23 GW of modern natural gas and geothermal generation, creating an unmatched platform to serve hyperscaler datacenter power demand.

FY2025 revenue reached $25.5B (+8.3% YoY) with adjusted EPS of $9.39. The company has guided 2026 adjusted EPS to $11.00-12.00 and committed to a 20% CAGR in base earnings through 2029. Calpine is expected to deliver 20%+ EPS accretion, with Q1 2026 EBITDA consensus at $2.02B — a 139% sequential lift from Q4 2025.

The datacenter PPA pipeline is the crown jewel: Microsoft has a 20-year PPA backing the Crane Clean Energy Center restart (835 MW), Meta secured 1.1 GW from the Clinton nuclear plant, and CyrusOne contracted 380 MW. Nuclear license renewals (Clinton to 2047, Dresden to 2049/2051) extend the earnings runway for decades. A $5B buyback provides capital return support. At ~$307 per share, the stock sits 24% below the average analyst target of $381, creating an attractive entry point after the guidance-driven selloff.

Datacenter Power Strategy

Nuclear Fleet: Largest US nuclear operator with ~22 GW — premium baseload, zero-carbon power ideally suited for 24/7 datacenter loads. Fleet capacity factor consistently above 93%.

Microsoft PPA: Crane Clean Energy Center (formerly TMI Unit 1) restart — 835 MW backed by a 20-year power purchase agreement. $1.6B restart investment. PJM interconnection flagged for potential 2031 delay (key risk).

Meta PPA: Clinton nuclear plant — 1.1 GW contracted under long-term agreement. Plant license renewed through 2047.

CyrusOne: 380 MW contracted for datacenter operations.

Calpine Synergies: 23 GW of gas and geothermal capacity adds flexibility for peaking and grid-balancing roles that complement nuclear baseload. Includes The Geysers — world's largest geothermal complex.

License Extensions: Clinton extended to 2047, Dresden Units 2 & 3 extended to 2049/2051 — $370M invested in relicensing. Secures decades of nuclear generation.

Risks & Concerns

• Crane interconnection delay: PJM flagged potential delay to 2031 for grid interconnection — threatens the Microsoft PPA and the $1.6B restart investment

• Calpine integration: Absorbing 2,300 employees, 60+ power plants, and rising interest expense from $16.4B acquisition financing

• Guidance disappointment: 2026 midpoint ($11.50 EPS) below initial Street consensus ($11.73) — caused 7% single-day stock drop

• Nuclear operational risk: Unplanned outages across 22 GW fleet can materially impact quarterly earnings

• Regulatory and policy risk: Clean energy subsidy changes, capacity market reforms, PJM interconnection rule changes

• Debt load: $7.3B LT debt post-Calpine — interest expense elevated while integration proceeds

Peer Comparison

vs. Vistra Energy (VST): Both operate nuclear fleets serving datacenter demand. Constellation is significantly larger (55 GW vs ~38 GW) and has the industry's largest nuclear fleet. Vistra has stronger ERCOT positioning and Comanche Peak nuclear assets. Constellation's Calpine deal creates superior scale but also integration risk that Vistra doesn't face.

vs. NextEra Energy (NEE): NextEra is the world's largest renewable energy developer with a utility (FPL) anchor. Constellation has a larger nuclear fleet and more direct datacenter PPA exposure. NextEra offers defensive utility characteristics while Constellation is a higher-beta datacenter power play.

vs. GE Vernova (GEV): GEV supplies the turbines and equipment; Constellation owns and operates the generation fleet. Complementary rather than competitive — Constellation is a GEV customer for its gas turbine fleet. Different risk profiles: GEV is an equipment/backlog story, CEG is a power generation/PPA story.
